CHAPTER 2

Review of Related Literature and Systems

This chapter presents the review of related literature and systems that provide the foundation for the development of the Smart Neighborhood Maintenance Request and Response System. The related literature includes concepts, theories, and findings gathered from books, journals, and periodicals that are significant to the study. The related systems include discussions of technologies, platforms, and applications similar to the proposed system. This review also identifies the gaps in existing studies and systems, which the present study aims to address by integrating AI-based classification to enhance community request and response efficiency.

Related Literature:

Aguilar (2021) provides a systematic review of AI techniques applied to energy management in smart buildings, addressing the integration of AI for automation and efficiency gains in urban infrastructure. The theoretical framework is rooted in AI-driven optimization and decision-making to enhance system responsiveness. This study supports the role of AI in automating infrastructure management, aligning with the research objective to use AI classification to improve maintenance request handling. The source is open access from ScienceDirect.

Alkhereibi et al. (2025) classify global smart cities using machine learning on livability and technology indices, offering a framework for evaluating AI integration in urban systems. The study's theoretical underpinning lies in data-driven urban performance metrics and AI-enhanced governance. It exposes gaps in how AI-driven classification can optimize municipal services, supporting the present study's aim to streamline public infrastructure maintenance through machine learning classification. Accessible via ScienceDirect.

Fang et al. (2023) review AI applications in waste management within smart cities, emphasizing deep learning and sensor use for automated classification and predictive maintenance. Their theoretical approach includes machine learning to optimize urban service operations. They highlight gaps in manual waste classification inefficiencies, supporting the present study's AI-based automation goals. The article is available on PubMed Central.

Kabir et al. (2021) discuss explainable AI (XAI) in smart city applications, focusing on transparency and trust in AI-based public services. The theoretical basis incorporates human-centered AI design to promote user confidence and system accountability. This supports the study's goal to use AI while ensuring the public can engage transparently with infrastructure systems. The source is open access on arXiv.

Bayashot (2024) examines AI-powered mobile apps' transformative role in urban ecosystems, highlighting improvements in public service delivery through data-driven decision-making. The study builds on the framework of digital governance and citizen engagement. It addresses gaps in integrating AI with urban service usability, underscoring the current study's emphasis on enhancing resident interaction via web platforms. Readable freely on SCIRP.

A Smart City-enabled Infrastructure Asset Management framework by Misuraca et al. (2020) highlights how municipal managers can integrate AI and smart city concepts to optimize asset maintenance. The framework draws on system engineering and IT governance theories. This supports the prototype system's administrative dashboard for data-driven prioritization. The report is free from the European Commission website.

Henk and Henk (no date) present a structured literature review on public sector AI adoption with an ADO (Antecedents, Decisions, Outcomes) framework, emphasizing efficiency and transparency drivers in government AI use. The review identifies gaps in tailored AI solutions for local governance, reinforcing the study's objective to improve local public infrastructure maintenance. The paper is openly accessible from Nord University.

A web-based road management and public complaint registry study (2022) demonstrates how digital platforms improve transparency and responsiveness in urban infrastructure repair. Drawing from e-governance theories, it identifies the gap of manual complaint systems and supports the use of AI for automated categorization. The paper is freely accessible via IJERT.

Johnson et al. (2021) discuss the use of digital platforms for community-based environmental monitoring, grounding their work in participatory governance and data management theory. They note sustainability and long-term usage as challenges, highlighting the importance of user-friendly reporting platforms. This aligns with the current study's focus on community engagement through a web interface. The article is open on Oxford Academic.

Chen and Das (2023) propose AI maintenance from a robustness perspective, paralleling vehicle maintenance with AI model calibration and updating. Their theoretical contribution is essential for maintaining reliable AI in public systems, underlying the study's AI classification sustainability concerns. This preprint is openly available on arXiv.

Gupta (2025) explores AI-powered predictive maintenance frameworks in Smart City IoT systems, applying machine learning for real-time operational reliability. This approach enhances resource allocation and reduces unplanned downtime, directly supporting the study's AI prioritization aim. The article is available in Research Annals and openly accessible.

The S&P Global report (2024) on AI-powered smart cities discusses increasing AI application for infrastructure development and data analysis, emphasizing smart city operational improvements. The report's insights support digital transformation trends that justify the use of AI-driven systems for neighborhood maintenance. The report is publicly accessible.

Maciá-Lillo et al. (2025) investigate serverless edge-cloud architectures for knowledge management in smart cities, based on distributed computing and AI integration theories. This work supports the system's need for scalable, real-time data processing for maintenance management. The paper is available via Nature Communications open access.

The AI Watch report (2020) proposes a framework for AI use in public services, focusing on risks, impacts, and integration into government workflows. The theoretical foundation is in public management and AI governance. It identifies adoption challenges, validating the study's approach to prototype testing and phased implementation. Accessible through the EU AI Watch portal.

ManWinWin (2024) describes web-based Computerized Maintenance Management Systems (CMMS) with QR code integration for efficient maintenance reporting. Their framework follows technology acceptance model theories to improve user participation. This supports the study's web platform design to reduce manual error and improve communication. The content is free on their official website.

Hodson (2024) explores urban infrastructure reconfiguration via digital platforms, grounded in platform urbanism and transition theory. The research examines rapid digital mobility platforms, echoing the need for integrated, AI-supported infrastructure maintenance platforms. This enhances the study's context on smart urban systems and data collaboration. The article is open access on ScienceDirect.

Mai (2021) presents continual learning in image classification, focusing on AI adaptability and performance metrics. The theoretical background is in machine learning lifecycle and real-time data model adaptation, directly supporting the AI classification prototype's need to improve accuracy dynamically. The thesis is freely available online.

Whyte (2016) reviews urban infrastructure systems and integration, highlighting data analytics and IoT as enablers of smart city infrastructure management. Though slightly older than 10 years, the content is widely cited and foundational for understanding integrated urban maintenance frameworks. The overview is accessible on ScienceDirect.

Dos Santos et al. (2025) investigate drivers and barriers of AI adoption in smart city systems, applying technology acceptance and innovation diffusion theories. Their findings on adoption challenges support the study's attention to usability and stakeholder buy-in for neighborhood maintenance AI systems. The article is on ScienceDirect, open access.

Kulal et al. (2024) analyze AI adoption impact on public service delivery efficiency in India, grounded in public administration and digital transformation theory. They highlight measurable gains in responsiveness and resource utilization, supporting the system's objective to improve maintenance service delivery. The paper is freely available on ScienceDirect.

Related System

Theissler (2021) surveys predictive maintenance systems enabled by machine learning, categorizing key algorithms and their roles in operational optimization. The study establishes the theoretical basis of ML models in failure prediction and resource allocation. The challenge of real-time integration and accuracy in complex urban systems supports the study's AI classification objective to enhance maintenance prioritization. The article is freely accessible via ScienceDirect.

Singgih (2024) reviews machine learning in predictive maintenance, emphasizing supervised learning models such as SVM and random forests to predict and classify equipment failures. Its technical relevance lies in real-time anomaly detection and decision support. The limitation of training data scarcity aligns with the current study's prototype training dataset scope. The paper is open at a public conference repository.

Raheem (2024) explores integrating machine learning with predictive maintenance focusing on sustainability and operational efficiency. The study uses supervised learning and time-series data analysis, foundational for automating maintenance request classification. Highlighted adoption challenges in Industry 4.0 parallel the study's future system scaling concerns. This master's thesis is open access.

BIAD Project (year not specified) details a database management system for local government units designed for socio-economic data integration to support decision-making. The project exemplifies database-driven governance frameworks relevant to developing the administrative dashboard of the study system. Data consistency and inter-municipal synchronization challenges pointed out support the study's design considerations. It is a public government document.

The 2025 Artificial Intelligence Index Report presents advances in AI capabilities, emphasizing performance improvements and real-world deployment trends in smart urban services. This work supports understanding the technical feasibility of integrating high-performing AI models in maintenance systems. It notes continued progress limitations like logical reasoning gaps, relevant to the prototype's classification accuracy considerations. The report is freely available.

Hilaris Publisher (2024) describes advances in computer vision-based civil infrastructure monitoring that leverage AI algorithms and drones for defect detection. The technical basis integrates image processing with predictive analytics, complementing the study's AI image classification module. The need for data fusion and real-time analytics underscores challenges the study aims to address. The paper is freely accessible.

A 2025 Vietnamese journal article presents research on digital databases for urban technical infrastructure management highlighting data accuracy, centralized monitoring, and decision support. The theoretical link is in database system design to enhance administrative operations. The high costs and data synchronization issues noted constitute gaps the prototype seeks to mitigate by focused scope and incremental development. The article is open access.

LLumin (2025) explains AI-powered predictive maintenance using machine learning and IoT data for timely asset failure forecasts, emphasizing the shift from reactive to data-driven maintenance. This technical concept underpins the study's approach to automate maintenance request categorization and prioritization. The challenge of balancing data complexity with operational benefits parallels the study objectives. The content is freely accessible.

Labeller (2024) reviews leading ML model serving platforms such as Kubeflow and MLflow, which support the entire machine learning lifecycle including deployment and monitoring. These platforms offer modularity and scalability, providing a technical foundation for integrating AI classification in web-based systems. The complexity of setup and resource demands align with challenges faced in prototype implementation. The article is open source.

Facilio (2025) critiques traditional maintenance ticketing systems focusing on inflexible workflows, data silos, and escalating backlogs. The technical study supports the current research's inclusion of AI-driven prioritization and real-time status tracking to overcome these deficiencies. The review highlights the imperative for scalable, automated, and integrated systems. The article is freely accessible on their site.

Setyawati et al. (2021) demonstrate a positive impact of web-based management information systems on service efficiency through system accessibility and digital integration. The study's theoretical premise of digital transformation in public services supports the study's web interface for resident reporting and dashboard functionalities. The paper is openly available.

Collins (2021) conducts a systematic literature review on AI research in information systems, offering a foundation for AI's evolving role in enterprise and governance systems. The review outlines gaps in transparency and trust in AI applications, supporting the study's transparent administrative dashboard design. The journal article is free access.

Spencer Jr. (2019) overviews computer vision advances for civil infrastructure condition assessment, showing how image analytics improve inspection speed and reliability. The research provides practical algorithms relevant to the prototype's image classification system. Limitations in image dataset variability mirror challenges in system accuracy. The publication is open access.

Cities Alliance (year not specified) provides an innovative data toolkit for city management that emphasizes data harmonization and stakeholder coordination. This tool relates to the study's goal of integrating analytics for decision-making and community engagement. The toolkit is freely accessible online.

Oracle (2024) discusses AI in predictive maintenance, highlighting faster failure prediction using advanced data analytics and integrating IoT sensor data. The article underpins the study's technical approach to real-time monitoring and predictive classification. The document is publicly available.

eWeek (2024) lists top machine learning platforms like Google Vertex AI and H2O.ai, focusing on usability, scalability, and integration. The platforms' features support the study's AI model deployment decisions, emphasizing open-source benefits and implementation challenges. The review is free to read.

Limble CMMS (2025) examines work request system efficiency, emphasizing simplified workflows and standardized submissions to reduce delay and backlog accumulation. This supports the research's web platform design focusing on user-friendly, standardized reporting to optimize response management. The article is openly accessible.

Kingston (2000) though older, describes early web-based environmental decision support systems that continuously update public input databases—a conceptual precursor to AI-driven maintenance systems. It indicates gaps in real-time integration and multi-user coordination that the new system aims to solve. The paper is free on ScienceDirect.

Rashid et al. (2024) review AI's revolutionary impact across industries, focusing on implementation frameworks and challenges such as data privacy and algorithmic bias. Their findings support the study's cautious phased prototype development approach. The review is open access.

Singgih (2024) (another paper) details the limitations of ML in predictive maintenance, particularly model accuracy and complexity in real-world applications. This technical insight informs the study's deliberate approach to basic AI model training on limited datasets, highlighting the necessity for incremental improvement. The literature is open at the conference repository.
